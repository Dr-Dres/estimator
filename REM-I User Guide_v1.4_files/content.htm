    ‌3 Overview‌


    ‌System Overview‌

    The REM-I platform combines high-resolution, brightfield single-cell imaging, cutting-edge AI, and six-way label-free sorting for high-dimensional morphology analysis, while maintaining cell viability for downstream analysis.

    The system consists of three components:

  • The REM-I benchtop instrument and computer running the Controller software that allows you to operate the instrument and set up and run imaging and sorting experiments.

  • Deepcell’s Human Foundation Model (HFM) extracts features from diverse cell images without prior knowledge of specific cell types, cell preparation, or other application-specific markers for an unbounded approach to hypothesis generation and testing.

  • Axon Data Suite enables real-time exploration of data from the REM-I instrument.


    ‌Workflow Overview‌

    A typical workflow consists of the following:

  • Single-cell suspension is loaded on the REM-I.

  • Cells are imaged on the microfluidic chip.

  • Morphologic identification and classification is performed using HFM technology.

  • If sorting is desired, cells are sorted into wells on the chip for downstream analysis.

  • Axon Data Suite enables real-time exploration of data.


‌Instrument Overview‌

The REM-I benchtop instrument uses high-throughput imaging to characterize, classify, and sort cells based on morphological analyses. The instrument uses a microfluidic chip, which allows for the input and flow of cells in suspension. The high-speed system collects brightfield images of cells as they flow through the chip. High-resolution images capture sub-cellular and sub-nuclear features of the cells in high contrast. A laser-based system tracks cells in real-time to assist with imaging and sorting. Deepcell’s Human Foundation Model (HFM) provides a framework for analysis, allowing you to select cell populations for sorting. Our sorting chip has six collection wells for collecting the sorted cells.

‌Software Overview‌

The REM-I Controller software allows you to control the instrument and carry out imaging and sorting workflows. The software runs on a tower computer and is displayed on the instrument’s display screen.

The Controller software has two main tabs—Workflow and Hardware. The Workflow tab is the primary tab used to operate and maintain the instrument, as well as run imaging and sorting workflows. This guide focuses on describing the Workflow tab. Specific controls for operating the instrument appear on screen in the workflow steps where that function is necessary. For example, during the workflow you will be prompted by the software to open the main instrument door and move the stage to the loading position when it’s time to load a chip.

A sub-level row of tabs within the Workflow tab present a series of steps that you will follow when setting up and performing an imaging or sorting run. Each tab consists of a gray Start bar at the top of the tab. See Figure 3 on page 19. Click Start to initiate the tab, then follow the steps on the tab.

Most tabs display a Stop bar that you click when you are finished at that tab. When you have completed all the steps within a tab, click the next tab to advance to the next steps in the workflow.

‌Chips‌

Two chip types are available to image and sort cells. An imaging chip is used to capture images for morphologic characterization. A sorting chip is used to sort cells based on morphology.

Imaging and Sorting buffers flow through the instrument's fluid lines to the chip. The Imaging buffer pushes cells toward the center of the chip channel during imaging and sorting runs. The Sorting buffer carries cells of interest to the collection wells. Any waste and uncollected sample flow to the waste bottle. If the negative output tube is selected, uncollected cells flow to the negative output tube. 10% bleach solution and DI water wash the instrument post-run to minimize cross-contamination in subsequent experiments. Bleach solution and DI water flow to the waste bottle.


Figure 4. Imaging chip

‌Chip Handling‌

  • Chips are single-use only.

  • Store chips at the recommended temperatures:

    • Store Imaging chips at room temperature for up to 6 months.

    • Store Sorting chips at –20°C for up to 6 months.

  • Do not touch the sorting field of view on the chip.

  • If there is a need to clean the chip, wipe only the bottom glass surface with 70% ethanol and Kimwipes.

  8. ‌Human Foundation Model (HFM)‌


    ‌Overview‌

    Deepcell’s Human Foundation Model (HFM) is an innovative hybrid architecture that merges self- supervised learning with morphometrics (computer vision). This synergy enables the extraction of 115-dimensional embeddings that describe cell morphology from high-resolution cell images captured on the REM-I instrument. Because the HFM is a foundation model, it is endowed with generalization capabilities, unlocking the potential for comprehensive exploration of morphological variations between cells.

    The self-supervised nature of the HFM means that the training data does not require labeling of specific features to be learned, enabling users to take advantage of scale in place of meticulous labeling. Simultaneously, the computer vision facet of the HFM extracts features that signify measurable concepts, enhancing model interpretability. This includes aspects such as cell size, shape, texture, and intensity, which can greatly aid researchers in deciphering cellular characteristics and functions.


    ‌Decoding the HFM: Interpreting and Utilizing Results‌

    The output of the HFM is an embedding vector for each cell in a sample. These vectors contain 115 dimensions (51 morphometric dimensions and 64 AI dimensions), painting a holistic picture of each cell's morphology.

    The Axon data suite, an integral component of the REM-I platform, serves as the hub for storing, visualizing, and analyzing the output data. The embedding vectors are projected onto morphology Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projections (UMAPs), representing the degree of morphological similarity between cells. Within Axon, researchers can seamlessly navigate cell images, compare populations across dimensions, and even export graphs and images for further exploration. Then, users can geometrically select cell populations for sorting in up to 6 wells for further molecular or functional analyses.


  9. ‌Sample Preparation Guidelines‌‌


    ‌Preparing Samples for Morpholomic Analysis‌

    Using deep learning and computer vision the REM-I platform enables high-dimensional single cell morphology discoveries and characterization. This method of examining morphology can detect very subtle changes to cells such as the ones occurring with overconfluence, medium toxicity, or other perturbations. It is, therefore, very important to minimize the introduction of bias associated with cell preparation affecting morphology and to design experiments with only one variable of interest different between samples.

    A run on the Deepcell REM-I platform starts with a single cell suspension. To ensure a successful run for acquiring high-quality cell images or sorting of high-quality cells for downstream processing, cells should display good cell health with little to no cell debris. The REM-I platform is compatible with many human cell types, including cell lines and primary samples. Depending on the sample source and research question, initial pre-processing may be required to enrich cells of interest and/or remove unwanted cells, cellular aggregates and debris. Examples include body fluids, solid tissue, tumors, and adherent cells. Protocols used to generate single-cell suspensions for other single-cell modalities (flow cytometry, single-cell genomics, etc.) are generally compatible with the REM-I platform. Note: cells must be resuspended in the provided REM-I Sample buffer before loading on the REM-I instrument.

    This document provides tips, tricks, and best practices for preparing different sources of human cells and quality assessment recommendations for a successful run on the REM-I platform.

    For additional support preparing your samples, visit the Deepcell Resources page at deepcell.com/ resources or contact support@deepcellbio.com.

      ‌Beginning Quality Assessments‌

      For cryopreserved samples, let the cells recover fully before inspecting for compatibility with the REM-I platform. In all cases, inspect samples immediately before running on the REM-I platform. Depending on cell type, aggregates may form or viability may significantly decrease when cells are kept in suspension for a prolonged period of time before use. Therefore, cell suspensions should be analyzed as soon as possible after cell preparation (<1 hr). If it is not possible to analyze cell suspensions within an hour of preparation, we recommend fixing cells.

      ‌Cells from Dissociated Tumor and Normal Tissues‌

      Dissociated tumor cells pose unique challenges due to their heterogeneity, cell composition, and fragility. In general, tumor cells are very sensitive to tumor dissociation and tend to be less robust than infiltrating immune cells. Once dissociated, tumor cells need to be processed on the REM-I instrument very quickly to prevent tumor cell death. The same principle of dissociation applies for normal tissue cells.


      Cell Source

    • Obtain fresh tumor tissue and ensure minimal ischemic time to preserve cell viability.

    • Select appropriate mechanical and enzymatic dissociation methods based on the tissue type of origin.


      Dissociation Protocol

    • Optimize dissociation time to balance cell yield and viability.

    • Use slow speed centrifugation (150 g) to remove dead cells and smaller debris.

    • Filter the dissociated cells with a 40-□m* cell strainer to remove large debris and clumps.


      Cell Viability Maintenance

    • Keep cells on ice throughout the dissociation process.

    • Use a serum-containing medium supplemented with protease inhibitors to minimize cell stress.


      ‌Cell Lines‌

      Cell lines offer a more homogenous cell population compared to cells derived from tissue but still require careful preparation.


      Cell Culture Conditions

    • Maintain cells in logarithmic growth phase for optimal results.

    • Use the medium according to the manufacturer's recommendations


      Harvesting Cells

    • Use enzymatic or non-enzymatic methods based on cell adhesion properties.

    • Avoid over-confluent (>80%) cultures to prevent nutrients in the media from becoming depleted and cell clumping.


      Resuspending Cells

    • Resuspend cells gently using wide bore pipette tips and pipetting slowly to avoid perturbing cell integrity and viability.

      ‌Adherent Cells‌

      Adherent cells require additional steps to ensure detachment without compromising cell viability.


      Detachment Methods

    • Use cell dissociation solutions or enzymes specific to the cell type.

    • Optimize detachment time to balance yield and viability.


      Cell Washing

    • Rinse adherent cells gently using wide bore pipette tips to prevent cell loss.

    • Pipette up and down slowly to prevent clumping.


      Resuspension

    • Filter the cell suspension with a 40-□m* cell strainer to remove aggregates.

    • In some cases, it is possible to add a ROCK inhibitor to maintain cell viability after the cells are detached.

    • Verify cell viability and cell count after detachment and washing steps using a hemocytometer or an automated cell counter.


      ‌Organoids‌

      Organoids present a 3D structure, requiring specialized techniques for proper preparation.


      Isolation and Culture

    • Isolate organoids carefully to maintain their structural integrity.

    • Culture organoids in appropriate media and matrices to preserve their architecture.


      Dissociation and Single Cell Preparation

    • Use mechanical dissociation or enzymatic methods for organoid dissociation.

    • Optimize the dissociation process to achieve single cell suspensions.

    • Optimize dissociation time to maximize cell viability.

    • Use slow speed centrifugation (150 g) to remove dead cells and smaller debris

    • Filter the dissociated cells with a 40 □m* cell strainer to remove large debris and clumps.


      Viability Considerations

    • Verify cell viability and cell count after dissociation using an automated cell counter.

    • Handle organoid-derived cells gently to avoid clumping.

      ‌Additional Considerations for REM-I Workflows‌

      Cell Size

      The REM-I instrument accepted cell size range is between 6 and 25 □m*. The cell sizes are measured with cells in suspension, which might differ from cells in a tissue culture dish as they tend to ball up upon dissociation. To evaluate the cell size, it is recommended to use a hemocytometer.


      Cell Debris and Aggregates (and Dead Cells)

      The amount of cell debris and dead cells in cell suspension will vary depending on the tissue of origin and the method used to dissociate tissues, tumors, transplants and organoids, and cells in culture. Cell debris and dead cells tend to be very sticky and can clog the filter region of the REM-I chip. Centrifuge at slow speed (150 x g) to remove dead cells and small cell debris. If a sample contains a high percentage (>40%) of small cell debris (<6 □m*), it can adversely affect sorting yield and purity. If large cell clumps are present, filter the cell suspension using cell strainers.

      Fixation

      When it is difficult to analyze cell morphology within an hour of sample preparation, we recommend fixing the cells to image at a more convenient time.

    • We recommend testing different concentrations of PFA (1–5%) and fixation times. Cell morphology of different cell types is affected differently when exposed to fixative.

    • We recommend comparing the morphology of fresh cells and fixed cells under the microscope.

    • We also recommend evaluating morphology in the context of storage conditions to minimize morphology degradation over time.

    • If you are fixing cells, make sure that the downstream assays you are planning are compatible with fixed cells.
